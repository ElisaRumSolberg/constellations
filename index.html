About Constellations

Constellations are groups of stars that form specific patterns in the sky and are associated with certain figures. Humanity has studied the night sky for thousands of years, connecting these stars to shapes and mythological figures.

In ancient civilizations such as Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, and China, people discovered star patterns and gave them names. The earliest records of constellations date back to around 3000 B.C. on Mesopotamian tablets. Greek astronomer Ptolemy, who laid the foundations of Western astronomy, listed 48 constellations in his work "Almagest" and created a systematic sky map.

In addition to their mythological meanings, constellations played an important role in navigation and agriculture. Well-known constellations like Ursa Major (the Great Bear), Ursa Minor (the Little Bear), and Orion have served as guides in the night sky for travelers. Today, there are 88 constellations officially recognized by the International Astronomical Union. These constellations divide the sky into specific regions, making astronomical observations easier.

Constellation Guide

Ursa Major (The Great Bear)

Known for the “Big Dipper” pattern, Ursa Major is easily recognizable in the northern hemisphere. Consisting of seven bright stars, it serves as a guide to finding the North Star. Ancient sailors used this constellation for navigation.

Ursa Minor (The Little Bear)

Ursa Minor includes the North Star, which is a reference point for finding the north direction. It has a shape similar to the Great Bear but is smaller. It has been a fixed point in the sky for finding the north since ancient times.

Orion (The Hunter)

Orion is known as the hunter in mythology, easily recognizable by the three bright stars in its belt. Orion’s brightest stars are Betelgeuse (reddish) and Rigel (bluish-white). It appears as a distinct shape in the winter sky.

Cassiopeia

Shaped like the letter W or M, it can be seen throughout the night in the northern hemisphere. In mythology, it represents Queen Cassiopeia of Ethiopia, who was placed in the sky, turned upside down as a punishment for her vanity.

Scorpius (The Scorpion)

Scorpius becomes visible in the southern sky during summer, known for its curved tail and the bright, reddish star Antares. In mythology, it is known as the scorpion that killed the hunter Orion.

Gemini (The Twins)

The constellation represents the mythical twins Castor and Pollux, with the two stars of the same names marking their heads. Gemini is part of the zodiac and is visible in the winter sky.

Leo (The Lion)

Leo appears in the spring sky and resembles a lion shape. Its brightest star, Regulus, represents the lion’s heart. In mythology, Leo is associated with the Nemean Lion slain by Hercules. .

Taurus (The Bull)

Taurus is known for its V shape and the bright star Aldebaran. In Greek mythology, it represents Zeus transforming into a bull to abduct Europa. It also includes the famous Pleiades and Hyades star clusters.p>

Sagittarius (The Archer)

Sagittarius resembles an archer holding a bow and faces the center of the Milky Way Galaxy. It forms a “teapot” shape and contains many stars, making it a part of the zodiac.

Aquarius (The Water Bearer)

A member of the zodiac, Aquarius is depicted as a figure pouring water. It has been associated with water since ancient times and spans a wide area of the sky, becoming visible in autumn.

Pisces (The Fish)

Pisces is one of the zodiac constellations and represents two fish tied together. It is said to symbolize the myth of Aphrodite and her son Eros transforming into fish to escape a monster. It forms a V shape in the sky.

Corona Borealis (The Northern Crown)

This small crown-shaped constellation is visible in the northern hemisphere. In Greek mythology, it represents the crown given to Ariadne by the god Dionysus.

Aquila (The Eagle)

Known for its brightest star Altair, Aquila becomes prominent in the summer sky. In mythology, it represents Zeus’ eagle and is part of the “Summer Triangle,” along with Lyra and Cygnus constellations.

Bootes (The Herdsman)

Known as the herdsman, Bootes contains the bright star Arcturus, one of the brightest stars in the sky. It can be observed in spring and has been seen in ancient tales as a figure protecting agriculture and farming.

Pegasus (The Winged Horse)

The Pegasus constellation forms a large square in the sky, representing the mythical winged horse Pegasus. It becomes prominent in autumn, creating a shape known as the “Great Square.”
